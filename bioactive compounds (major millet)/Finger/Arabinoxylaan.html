Arabinoxylan

Arabinoxylan is the primary non-starch polysaccharide present in the cell walls of cereal grains, with rye grains being the richest source followed by wheat and barley grains. Finger millet also contains arabinoxylans, which exist in both water-extractable and water-unextractable forms with a molecular weight of about 139.9-140 kDa. Finger millet arabinoxylans are highly branched and complex in structure, containing terminally linked arabinose, uronic and ferulic acids, galactose, and small amounts of xylose. The structure of finger millet arabinoxylans is similar to other cereal arabinoxylans, with a linear backbone of β-d-xylopyranosyl residues linked through a (1→4) glycosidic bond and attached residues of α-l-arabinopyranosyl at carbon position 3 or 2, or both. The polysaccharide is highly branched, with most arabinose and xylose residue substitutions at carbon position 3. Finger millet arabinoxylans also contain up to 10% of uronic acid in the form of glucuronic acid, which is linked to the xylose residues at C-2. Finger millet arabinoxylans have been shown to have high antioxidant activity due to the presence of bound phenolic acids, particularly ferulic acid, which can form cross-linkages by oxidation to adjacent arabinoxylan chains. These cross-linkages result in the formation of ferulate dimers or rarely triferulate residues, collectively known as feraxans, which possess high antioxidant properties. The presence of a high amount of uronic acid and molecular weight also contributes to their antioxidant activity. However, there is still limited research on the structural characteristics and associated activity of finger millet arabinoxylans.

Uses/ Applications

  • Diabetes.

    • Early research shows that eating food fortified with arabinoxylan for 5 weeks lowers blood sugar levels and insulin levels in people with type 2 diabetes. It also seems to improve blood sugar control.

  • Prediabetes

    • Early research shows that eating food fortified with arabinoxylan for 6 weeks lowers blood sugar levels and levels of a bloodfat called triglycerides.

  • A grouping of symptoms that increase the risk of diabetes, heart disease, and stroke (metabolic syndrome). Early research shows that eating a diet containing arabinoxylan does not seem to improve blood sugar or cholesterol levels in most people with metabolic syndrome.
  • Heart disease.
  • Constipation.
  • Weight loss.
  • Other conditions.
