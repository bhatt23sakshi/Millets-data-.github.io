Bioactive peptides

Bioactive Peptides (BP) are specific protein fragments that have a positive impact on body functions or conditions and may influence health. These organic substances are formed by amino acids joined by covalent bonds, also known as amide or peptide bonds. BP and proteins play important roles in the metabolic functions of living organisms and, consequently, in human health. They display hormone or drug-like activities and can be classified based on their mode of action as antimicrobial, anti-thrombotic, antihypertensive, opioid, immunomodulatory, mineral binding, and antioxidative. The activity of the peptides is determined by the amino acid composition and sequence once they are released from the precursor protein where they are encrypted. Proteins, which are the primary components of food, can be classified as endogenous or exogenous. Both plant and animal proteins are potential sources of a wide range of BP encrypted in their structure. BP share some structural features, including a peptide residue length between 2–20 amino acids and the presence of hydrophobic amino acids in addition to proline, lysine, or arginine groups. They have also shown to be resistant to the action of digestion peptidases, indicating their potential bioavailability in the body. While the correlation between structure and functional properties is not well established, the identification and characterization of BP in food sources have gained significant attention due to their potential health benefits. Therefore, the exploration of BP as a source of functional ingredients has the potential to lead to the development of new functional foods and nutraceuticals with various health benefits.

Applications

  • Nutraceutical

    • The use of nutraceuticals, which are naturally derived substances extracted from sources such as fruits, plants, lignocellulosic biomass, and algae, has gained significant attention in recent years due to their potential health benefits when incorporated into food or pharmaceutical formulations. Functional foods and nutraceuticals have been studied extensively for their role in disease prevention and human health improvement . The isolation and purification of functional peptides present in complex matrices containing numerous hydrolyzed protein fractions are essential. Traditional pressure-driven separation methods suffer from fouling issues and have low selectivity when separating similarly sized biomolecules (Bazinet and Firdaous, 2009). To obtain better-purified products, separation processes that use an electrical field as a driving force and porous membranes have been developed for biopeptide separation. More recently, electrodialysis with ultrafiltration membranes has been developed to fractionate acidic and basic peptides simultaneously using a conventional electrodialysis cell with ultrafiltration membranes replacing some of the ion exchange membranes (Bazinet and Firdaous, 2013). After separation and identification of biopeptides in three hypoallergenic infant milk formulas, it was confirmed that 24, 30, and 38 biopeptides were present in each formula. Many of these peptides were reported as ACE inhibitors, while others showed biological activities such as antihypertensive, anti-thrombotic, hypocholesterolemic, immunomodulatory, cytotoxic, antioxidant, antimicrobial, antigenic, or opioid properties .

  • Functional ingredients

    • BP are released from milk proteins during the fermentation or ripening of dairy products by microorganisms or through the hydrolysis of the purified proteins by enzymes. BP have several potential health benefits such as antihypertensive, antimicrobial, antioxidant, antitumour, and anti-inflammatory activities. They may be used in the formulation of functional foods, nutraceuticals, and drugs for health improvement. Alpha-lactalbumin is a globular protein found in mammalian milk and is a rich source of BP with several health-promoting properties.
